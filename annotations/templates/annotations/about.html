About Vogon and the epistemic web

In the humanities, making new knowledge involves reading and critically interpreting a wide range of texts: books, archives, articles, images, interviews, and news clippings. Scholars share their insights by arranging evidence and interpretation into books and articles that can in turn be read and critically interpreted by other scholars. Sadly, much of that literature is read by only a small number of people, and can be hard to find. It's even harder to validate research findings: scholars cite each others' work, but practices for citing primary sources like archives vary widely. Dozens of claims about historical actors or events might be covered by a single reference to an enormous archive.

What if we could do better? What if the sum of our research was not just a pile of papers, but a tangible network of historical actors, events, texts, and interpretations? What if we could find out who claimed that Louis Agassiz disagreed with Charles Darwin, and on the basis of what evidence, with the click of a mouse? Scholarly publishing has moved online. Our sources—even the dusty archives—are moving online. It's time to rethink the possibilities for making human knowledge in the digital age.

Building the Epistemic Web

VogonWeb is a web-based platform for encoding your interpretations of texts. The annotation interface allows you to tag words or phrases with the concepts to which you believe they refer, and bring those tags together into relational statements that you believe are supported by the text. These tags and relations comprise the epistemic web: a network of texts, interpretations, and the people who create those interpretations. As we build the epistemic web we are producing not only a rich knowledge-base for the humanities, but also documenting the scholarly process itself.

Technology

VogonWeb is one component in a constellation of web applications and services that comprise a distributed research infrastructure for digital and computational humanities.

VogonWeb is Python WSGI application developed using the Django web framework, and runs on the Heroku cloud platform.

How does it work?

One of the core ideas of the epistemic web is the ability to point to a specific piece of evidence for each interpretation that a user encodes. We tend to think about this in terms of printed or digital texts. For example:

  1. A user reads a passage, e.g. "Einstein was a theoretical physicist"
  2. The user form some interpretations, e.g. the user interprets the phrase "Einstein" as referring to the person Albert Einstein, the German-born man who lived from 1879 to 1955, and interprets the phrase "theoretical physicist" to refer to a general concept of a person who works in the field of theoretical physics.
  3. The user creates annotations for each of the phrases that she interprets, and links them together in a relation, e.g. that Albert Einstein was a theoretical physicist
  4. A second user who views the annotations can subsequently find the specific place where the passage interpreted by the first user as Albert Einstein is located.

What can I annotate?

In the first iteration of VogonWeb (up through version 0.4), we focused on annotating plain text documents. That is, texts without any kind of formatting or markup. For example, PDFs that have been processed using Optical Character Recognition, and HTML documents that have been scraped for their content. This was a logical starting-place, because it's easy to describe a location in a string of plain text, and many of our core users were already working with plain text that they had prepared for other workflows (e.g. topic modeling, Named Entity Recognition, relation extraction).

In the next minor version of VogonWeb (version 0.5), we will incorporate annotation of raw images (e.g. photos of archival documents) and markup (e.g. webpages). If you're interested in using these features, please get in touch!

In the future, we also hope to incorporate direct annotation of PDF documents. We've also been dreaming about audio and video annotation, but that's much further down the road.

What's all this good for, anyway?

Here are a few use-cases from real projects that have used or are currently using the Vogon/Quadriga annotation platform. VogonWeb is under constant development, and we need your input to make it even more wonderful. Please contact Erick to get involved.

Documenting Archival Research

"I am an historian of science studying plant science at agricultural research stations. In the archives I photograph a large number of hand-written and type-script letters and other documents. I want to keep track of who is mentioned in these documents and how they are related, so that I can develop a better understanding of how networks of people, institutions, organisms and other historical actors developed over time. I also want to be able to retrace my steps to find the specific places in documents that substantiate relationships between actors, and be able to quickly share my data with colleagues.”

Analyzing Functional Relationships in Texts

"As a philosopher of science, I am interested in how scientific terms (like ‘gene’, ‘organism’, or ‘mechanism’) are used by investigators in different fields and disciplines. Analyzing how terms are co-located in various texts (i.e. co-occurrence networks) gets me part of the way, but I want to go further and examine functional relationships among terms when they are used in different contexts. I need a way to encode those relationships that allows me to make quantitative comparisons of term-usage, without committing to a rigid ontology ahead of time. By looking at differences in term usage in the way, I hope to gain better insights into how concepts differ between research communities and change over time.

Collaborative Network Modeling Collaboratively, with Evidence

"I am studying collaboration networks in fields where multi-author publications are rare. I can’t use co-authorship to model those networks, so I want to annotate the acknowledgement sections of academic research papers to encode information about collaborations among scientists. Since I am working with thousands of documents, I want to enlist the help of students and colleagues to annotate these texts. That means that we need a controlled vocabulary of personal names so that we are all annotating the texts in the same way, and so that we can discriminate among people with similar names. We need to be able to see the specific passages of text that were used to encode relationships, so that we can validate our data.

Structuring Existing Knowledge for Discovery and Meta-analysis

"My colleagues and I have written a wide range of papers that have some overlap in the people, places, concepts, and events that we describe. We want a structured framework for looking at our work in aggregate, so that we can see precisely where our work overlaps. This will make it easier for our students to comprehend the landscape of existing scholarship in this field, and identify areas where further research may be fruitful.”
